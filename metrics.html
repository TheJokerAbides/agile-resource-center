Useful Agile Metrics

A practical guide to the most commonly used agile metrics — what they measure, how to calculate them, and the honest pros and cons of each. Metrics should inform conversations, not replace them.

⚡
Velocity
Story points completed per sprint
Flow Delivery
⌄
Velocity measures the average amount of work a team completes per sprint, expressed in story points. It is used to forecast how much work a team can take on in future sprints.
✓ Pros
  • Simple to track and understand
  • Useful for sprint forecasting and release planning
  • Helps teams identify sustainable pace over time
  • Encourages consistent estimation practices
✕ Cons
  • Story points are not comparable across teams
  • Can incentivise inflating estimates to look productive
  • Doesn't reflect quality — only quantity
  • Misleading when team composition changes
  • Should never be used to compare teams
Best used for: Internal sprint planning and release forecasting within a stable team. Treat it as a planning tool, not a performance measure.
🔄
Cycle Time
Time from work started → work delivered
Flow
⌄
Cycle time measures how long it takes for a work item to move from the moment active work begins to the moment it is delivered to the customer. It is a key Kanban metric for understanding team throughput and predictability.
✓ Pros
  • Objective — based on actual timestamps, not estimates
  • Highlights bottlenecks and blockers in the workflow
  • Great predictor of future delivery time
  • Improves with WIP limits and flow discipline
✕ Cons
  • Requires accurate tracking of when work truly starts
  • Outliers can skew averages significantly
  • Doesn't distinguish between item complexity
  • Can create pressure to rush at the expense of quality
Best used for: Kanban teams focused on flow efficiency. Use percentile ranges (e.g. 85th percentile) rather than averages to set realistic SLAs.
⏱️
Lead Time
Time from request created → work delivered
FlowProduct
⌄
Lead time measures the total elapsed time from when a work item is requested (entered the backlog) to when it is delivered. Unlike cycle time, it includes queue and wait time — making it the metric customers care about most.
✓ Pros
  • Reflects the customer's experience of responsiveness
  • Exposes time lost waiting in the backlog
  • Drives focus on reducing queue and approval delays
  • Useful for setting stakeholder expectations
✕ Cons
  • Longer than cycle time — can feel discouraging
  • Influenced heavily by backlog prioritisation, not just dev speed
  • Hard to improve without also improving upstream processes
Best used for: Understanding end-to-end responsiveness. Use alongside cycle time to diagnose where delays occur — in the queue or in active development.
📉
Sprint Burndown
Remaining work vs. ideal completion line over sprint
Delivery
⌄
A sprint burndown chart tracks how much work remains in a sprint over time, compared to the ideal linear reduction. It gives the team a daily visual on whether they are on track to meet their sprint goal.
✓ Pros
  • Provides an early warning of sprint risk
  • Encourages daily transparency within the team
  • Simple and visual — easy for all stakeholders to read
  • Promotes conversation in daily standups
✕ Cons
  • Can create a false sense of progress if stories are not broken down well
  • Doesn't capture quality or technical debt
  • Teams may defer difficult tasks to look better on the chart
  • Scope changes mid-sprint distort the picture
Best used for: Daily sprint tracking and early risk detection. Most valuable when combined with a sprint goal rather than used as a task completion tracker.
📦
Throughput
Number of items completed per time period
FlowDelivery
⌄
Throughput counts the number of work items a team completes in a given time period (e.g. per week, per sprint). Unlike velocity, it doesn't rely on story point estimates — making it a more objective measure of output.
✓ Pros
  • No estimation required — based on completed items
  • Comparable across teams when item sizes are similar
  • Useful for Monte Carlo forecasting
  • Immune to estimation inflation
✕ Cons
  • Treats all items as equal regardless of complexity
  • Requires consistent story sizing discipline to be meaningful
  • Can incentivise splitting stories artificially to boost numbers
Best used for: Flow-based teams using Kanban or #NoEstimates approaches. Combine with cycle time for a complete picture of delivery performance.
🐛
Escaped Defect Rate
Bugs found in production ÷ total bugs found
Quality
⌄
Escaped defects are bugs that make it past testing into production. The escaped defect rate measures the proportion of bugs found by real users rather than caught internally — a direct indicator of testing effectiveness and Definition of Done rigour.
✓ Pros
  • Directly reflects customer-facing quality
  • Motivates investment in testing and automation
  • Reinforces the importance of a strong DoD
  • Easy to tie to business impact
✕ Cons
  • Requires reliable production monitoring to capture accurately
  • Can discourage teams from releasing frequently
  • Severity of bugs not always captured in the count
  • Definition of "defect" needs team agreement
Best used for: Teams looking to improve release quality and test coverage. Track alongside total defect count and severity to get a full picture.
🎯
Sprint Goal Achievement Rate
Sprints where goal was met ÷ total sprints
DeliveryTeam health
⌄
Tracks how often a team successfully achieves the sprint goal they committed to at sprint planning. It shifts focus from task completion to outcome delivery — aligning the team around value rather than output.
✓ Pros
  • Focuses the team on outcomes over output
  • Encourages meaningful sprint goal setting
  • Better indicator of value delivery than velocity
  • Useful for retrospective conversations
✕ Cons
  • Only as good as the quality of the sprint goal itself
  • Teams may set weak goals to guarantee success
  • Binary — doesn't capture partial achievement
  • Requires discipline in goal-setting to be meaningful
Best used for: Teams who write meaningful sprint goals and want to measure outcome-focused delivery. Discuss in retrospectives when the rate drops.
😊
Team Happiness / Satisfaction
Survey score (1–10) tracked over time
Team health
⌄
A simple periodic survey asking team members to rate how they feel about their work, team, and processes. Made popular by the Spotify squad model, it provides early signals of morale and engagement issues before they affect delivery.
✓ Pros
  • Simple to run — even a single question provides signal
  • Surfaces morale issues early
  • Encourages psychological safety when done anonymously
  • Low effort, high value for Scrum Masters and coaches
✕ Cons
  • Subjective — hard to act on without follow-up
  • Can become noise if nothing changes after feedback
  • Social desirability bias in non-anonymous settings
  • Trend over time matters more than a single score
Best used for: Scrum Masters and agile coaches monitoring team wellbeing. Always pair with a follow-up question: "What's one thing that would improve your score?"
🚀
DORA Metrics
4 key DevOps / delivery performance indicators
DeliveryQuality
⌄
Developed by the DevOps Research and Assessment team (as featured in Accelerate), the four DORA metrics are: Deployment Frequency, Lead Time for Changes, Change Failure Rate, and Time to Restore Service. Together they measure both delivery speed and stability.
✓ Pros
  • Research-backed — proven to correlate with organisational performance
  • Balances speed (deployment frequency, lead time) with stability (failure rate, recovery)
  • Used by high-performing teams globally
  • Industry benchmarks available for comparison
✕ Cons
  • Requires mature CI/CD pipelines to measure accurately
  • Less relevant for teams not doing continuous deployment
  • Can be gamed if used for performance management
  • Four separate metrics — requires tooling to track well
Best used for: Engineering and platform teams with CI/CD pipelines. The gold standard for measuring DevOps performance — reference the Accelerate book for full context.
🧪
Code Coverage
Lines tested by unit tests ÷ total lines of code
QualityUse with caution
⌄
Code coverage measures what percentage of production code is exercised by automated tests. It is often used as a proxy for test quality, though the relationship between coverage and actual quality is more nuanced than a percentage suggests.
✓ Pros
  • Easy to measure with standard tooling
  • Identifies untested code paths
  • Useful as a baseline minimum threshold
  • Encourages test-driven development habits
✕ Cons
  • High coverage doesn't equal good tests — assertions can be missing
  • Teams optimise for the number, not the value of tests
  • Easy to game with low-value tests
  • Can create false confidence in test suite quality
Best used for: Setting a floor, not a ceiling. A coverage threshold (e.g. 80%) is a useful safety net, but always review test quality alongside coverage numbers.
💬
Net Promoter Score (NPS)
% Promoters − % Detractors
Product
⌄
NPS measures customer loyalty by asking: "How likely are you to recommend this product to a friend or colleague?" on a 0–10 scale. Respondents are categorised as Promoters (9–10), Passives (7–8), or Detractors (0–6). NPS = % Promoters − % Detractors.
✓ Pros
  • Simple single-question survey with industry benchmarks
  • Strong predictor of customer retention and growth
  • Easy to track over time and across releases
  • Universally understood by business stakeholders
✕ Cons
  • A single number rarely reveals why customers feel a certain way
  • Cultural differences affect scoring — less reliable across regions
  • Prone to survey timing bias (e.g. right after a good release)
  • Doesn't tell you what to fix
Best used for: Product and customer success teams tracking overall sentiment trends. Always follow up with open-ended questions to turn the score into actionable insights.
🗂️
Work in Progress (WIP)
Number of items actively being worked on
FlowTeam health
⌄
WIP measures how many work items are actively in progress at any given moment. Limiting WIP is a core principle of Kanban — reducing it improves flow, reduces context switching, and exposes process bottlenecks (Little's Law: Cycle Time = WIP ÷ Throughput).
✓ Pros
  • Directly linked to cycle time via Little's Law
  • Reducing WIP improves flow without changing team capacity
  • Forces prioritisation — teams must finish before starting
  • Makes bottlenecks and blockers immediately visible
✕ Cons
  • Teams often resist WIP limits as they feel constraining
  • Requires cultural change to enforce effectively
  • WIP limits alone don't fix root-cause bottlenecks
Best used for: Kanban and flow-based teams. Start by measuring current WIP before setting limits — aim for approximately 1–1.5x the number of team members actively working.
