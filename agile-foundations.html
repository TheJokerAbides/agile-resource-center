📜 History

The History of Agile

Agile did not emerge overnight. It grew out of decades of frustration with the heavyweight, document-driven approaches to software development that dominated the 1970s, 80s, and 90s — most notably the Waterfall model, which required teams to fully define requirements before writing a single line of code.

By the 1990s, practitioners were experimenting with lighter, more adaptive approaches. Methodologies like Extreme Programming (XP), Scrum, DSDM, Crystal, and Feature-Driven Development (FDD) were gaining traction independently — all sharing a common philosophy: deliver working software early, collaborate closely with customers, and embrace change rather than resist it.

1970
1970s
Waterfall dominates. Winston Royce's 1970 paper describes sequential software development — often cited as the origin of Waterfall, though Royce himself noted its risks.
1986
1986
The "rugby approach" emerges. Takeuchi and Nonaka publish "The New New Product Development Game" in Harvard Business Review — introducing the concept of cross-functional teams working in overlapping phases. This paper directly inspired the name "Scrum."
1995
1995
Scrum is formalised. Ken Schwaber and Jeff Sutherland present Scrum at OOPSLA. Simultaneously, Kent Beck develops Extreme Programming (XP) with practices like pair programming, TDD, and continuous integration.
2001
February 2001
The Agile Manifesto is born. Seventeen software practitioners meet at the Snowbird ski resort in Utah and produce the Agile Manifesto — four values and twelve principles that would define a movement.
2010
2010s
Agile goes mainstream. Scrum and Kanban spread beyond software into marketing, HR, and operations. Scaled frameworks (SAFe, LeSS, Nexus) emerge to address enterprise adoption challenges.
2020
2020s
Agile matures and reflects. The industry debates "cargo cult agile" and over-formalisation. A renewed focus on outcomes, team autonomy, and the spirit of the Manifesto over rigid process compliance.
📜 Manifesto

The Agile Manifesto

In February 2001, seventeen software practitioners gathered in Snowbird, Utah. They represented a range of lightweight methodologies — XP, Scrum, DSDM, Adaptive Software Development, Crystal, FDD, Pragmatic Programming, and more — yet found common ground in what they valued.

The result was a single page: the Agile Manifesto. It opens with a statement of intent and then articulates four value statements — each one not dismissing the right-hand side, but declaring that the left-hand side is valued more.

We are uncovering better ways of developing software by doing it and helping others do it. Through this work we have come to value: — Preamble to the Agile Manifesto, 2001
Individuals and interactions
over processes and tools
Working software
over comprehensive documentation
Customer collaboration
over contract negotiation
Responding to change
over following a plan

The Manifesto was signed by Kent Beck, Mike Beedle, Arie van Bennekum, Alistair Cockburn, Ward Cunningham, Martin Fowler, James Grenning, Jim Highsmith, Andrew Hunt, Ron Jeffries, Jon Kern, Brian Marick, Robert C. Martin, Steve Mellor, Ken Schwaber, Jeff Sutherland, and Dave Thomas.

⚠️ The Manifesto does not say processes, documentation, contracts, or plans have no value — only that the items on the left are valued more. This distinction matters.
💎 Values

Core Values

The four Manifesto values are not prescriptive rules — they are orientations. They guide decision-making when teams face trade-offs, helping them choose the path that better serves people and outcomes over process and paperwork.

🤝
Individuals & interactions
People and how they work together matter more than the tools and processes surrounding them.
✅
Working software
The primary measure of progress is software that actually works — not documents about software.
🤲
Customer collaboration
Ongoing partnership with customers produces better outcomes than negotiating requirements upfront and delivering months later.
🔄
Responding to change
The ability to adapt to changing requirements — even late in development — is a competitive advantage, not a disruption.

Beyond the Manifesto, many agile frameworks build on five Scrum values — which bring the abstract principles of agile into the day-to-day culture of a team:

💪
Commitment
Team members personally commit to achieving the goals of the Scrum Team.
🎯
Focus
Everyone focuses on the work of the sprint and the goals of the Scrum Team.
🔓
Openness
The team and stakeholders agree to be open about all the work and the challenges with performing it.
🙏
Respect
Team members respect each other as capable, independent people.
🦁
Courage
Team members have the courage to do the right thing and work on difficult problems.
🧭 Principles

The 12 Principles

Behind the four values sit twelve principles — published alongside the Manifesto in 2001. They translate the values into practical guidance for how agile teams should work, plan, collaborate, and improve.

  1. Our highest priority is to satisfy the customer through early and continuous delivery of valuable software.
  2. Welcome changing requirements, even late in development. Agile processes harness change for the customer's competitive advantage.
  3. Deliver working software frequently, from a couple of weeks to a couple of months, with a preference to the shorter timescale.
  4. Business people and developers must work together daily throughout the project.
  5. Build projects around motivated individuals. Give them the environment and support they need, and trust them to get the job done.
  6. The most efficient and effective method of conveying information to and within a development team is face-to-face conversation.
  7. Working software is the primary measure of progress.
  8. Agile processes promote sustainable development. The sponsors, developers, and users should be able to maintain a constant pace indefinitely.
  9. Continuous attention to technical excellence and good design enhances agility.
  10. Simplicity — the art of maximising the amount of work not done — is essential.
  11. The best architectures, requirements, and designs emerge from self-organising teams.
  12. At regular intervals, the team reflects on how to become more effective, then tunes and adjusts its behaviour accordingly.
💡 Principles 1, 3, and 7 all point to the same idea: deliver working software, often, as the true measure of progress. If your team isn't regularly shipping working increments, that's the first thing to address.
🏃 Scrum

Introduction to Scrum

Scrum is the most widely adopted agile framework. It provides a lightweight structure for teams to deliver value iteratively through short cycles called sprints — typically one to four weeks long. Scrum does not prescribe specific engineering practices; instead it defines a set of roles, events, and artefacts that create transparency, enable inspection, and allow the team to adapt.

At its core, Scrum rests on three pillars: Transparency (the work and its status are visible to everyone), Inspection (the team regularly examines progress toward the sprint goal), and Adaptation (the team adjusts when inspection reveals problems).

The three roles

🧭
Product Owner
Owns the Product Backlog, defines priorities, and is accountable for maximising the value of the product.
🛡️
Scrum Master
Serves the team by coaching Scrum practices, removing impediments, and facilitating events.
👩‍💻
Developers
The people who do the work. Cross-functional — able to deliver a usable increment every sprint.

The five events

EventPurposeTimebox
The SprintThe container for all other events. A fixed timebox during which a "Done" increment is created.1–4 weeks
Sprint PlanningThe team defines the sprint goal and selects work from the backlog to deliver.≤ 8 hrs (4-week sprint)
Daily ScrumA daily 15-minute event for developers to inspect progress and adapt the plan for the next 24 hours.15 minutes
Sprint ReviewThe team presents the increment to stakeholders and gathers feedback to update the backlog.≤ 4 hrs
Sprint RetrospectiveThe team inspects how it worked and identifies the most impactful improvements for the next sprint.≤ 3 hrs

The three artefacts

Product Backlog
An ordered list of everything that might be needed in the product. Owned by the Product Owner.
Sprint Backlog
The sprint goal, the selected backlog items, and the plan for delivering the increment. Owned by the developers.
Increment
The sum of all completed work during a sprint. Must meet the Definition of Done to be considered releasable.
📗 The Scrum Guide

The Scrum Guide

The Scrum Guide is the official and definitive description of Scrum, written and maintained by its co-creators Ken Schwaber and Jeff Sutherland. First published in 2010 and updated periodically since, it is intentionally short — the 2020 version is just 13 pages — capturing only what is essential to Scrum and nothing more.

Schwaber and Sutherland deliberately keep the Scrum Guide minimal. They describe Scrum as a framework — a container within which teams apply their own processes, techniques, and practices. Anything not in the Scrum Guide is not Scrum, which means teams have considerable freedom in how they implement it.

Scrum is a lightweight framework that helps people, teams and organisations generate value through adaptive solutions for complex problems. — The Scrum Guide, 2020

Key updates in the 2020 edition include: the Scrum Team replacing separate "Development Team" terminology, the sprint goal becoming a commitment of the Sprint Backlog, and a stronger emphasis on self-management over self-organisation.

📗 The Scrum Guide is free. Read it directly at scrumguides.org — it takes about 30 minutes and is the single most valuable thing a new practitioner can read.
