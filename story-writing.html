🌿 Lean

Lean Story Writing Principles

Lean story writing is about writing just enough, just in time. The goal is not comprehensive specifications — it's a shared understanding of user value that enables the team to build the right thing. Every word in a story should earn its place.

Stories are promises for a conversation, not a contract for delivery. — Alistair Cockburn
🎯
Value over completeness
A story exists to deliver user value. If you can't articulate the value, the story shouldn't exist.
⏱️
Just in time
Write detail when you need it, not months in advance. Requirements change — detailed stories written too early are often obsolete by the time they're built.
🤝
Conversation over documentation
The story card prompts a conversation between the team, the PO, and the business. The card is a reminder — not a requirements document.
🔪
Thin vertical slices
Slice stories to be as small as possible while still delivering end-to-end user value. Thin slices ship faster, reduce risk, and generate learning sooner.
👤
User language
Write in the language of the user, not the system. "User submits the form" not "POST request is sent to /api/orders".
🧪
Testability by design
If you can't write an acceptance criterion for it, the story is too vague. Testability is a quality signal, not an afterthought.
3️⃣ The 3 C's

The 3 C's

Ron Jeffries' 3 C's framework — Card, Conversation, Confirmation — describes the three components every user story must have to be complete. Together they define what a story is, not just what it says.

C
Card
The written story — a short description of the desired outcome on a card or ticket. It is intentionally brief because the detail lives in the conversation, not the card. The card is a reminder and a planning token.
C
Conversation
The ongoing discussion between the team, the PO, and the business — before, during, and after development. This is where requirements get clarified, assumptions get surfaced, and shared understanding is built. The conversation is the most important C.
C
Confirmation
The acceptance criteria — the conditions that confirm the story is done. Confirmation makes the story testable and creates a shared definition of "done" for this specific piece of work. No confirmation = no way to know when you're finished.
💡 If a story has a detailed Card but no Conversation has happened, you don't have a story — you have a specification. The value of a story comes from the shared understanding built through conversation, not from what's written on the card.
📝 Formats

Common User Story Formats

There is no single "correct" format for a user story. Different teams use different templates depending on context, audience, and what drives the best conversation. What matters is that the story captures who, what, and why — not which template you use.

⭐
Standard Format — As a / I want / So that
Most widely used · Good for all contexts
⌄
The classic story format. It explicitly captures the user (As a), the action or feature (I want), and the business value or reason (So that). The "So that" clause is the most important part — and the most frequently omitted.
As a [type of user],
I want [to perform some action / have some feature],
So that [I can achieve some goal or receive some value].
✓ Good example
As a registered customer, I want to save multiple delivery addresses to my account, so that I can check out faster when ordering to familiar locations.
✕ Weak example
As a user, I want to save addresses. ← No specific persona. No "so that". No value stated.
🎯
Job Story Format — When / I want / So I can
Alan Klement · Situational context · Jobs to be Done
⌄
Job Stories (from Alan Klement's Jobs to be Done framework) replace the persona with a situation or context. This avoids the assumptions baked into personas and focuses on the motivating circumstances that drive behaviour. Particularly powerful for product discovery.
When [situation / context / trigger],
I want to [motivation / action],
So I can [expected outcome].
✓ Good example
When I'm checking out on my phone and need to deliver to a new address, I want to type an address and have it auto-suggested by postcode, so I can complete checkout without frustrating typing errors.
🔍
Feature Injection — In order to / As a / I want
Chris Matts · Business-value first
⌄
Feature Injection (from Chris Matts) flips the standard format to lead with the business value. This forces the author to justify the feature before describing it — a powerful check against building things for the wrong reasons. Useful when stories are being written from a business stakeholder perspective.
In order to [achieve business value / outcome],
As a [role / persona],
I want [feature / capability].
✓ Good example
In order to reduce checkout abandonment on mobile, as a returning customer, I want my saved addresses to auto-populate the delivery form.
⚡
Lightweight Format — Plain English
No template · Experienced teams · Direct
⌄
Experienced agile teams sometimes drop the template entirely and write stories as plain, concise statements of user need. This works well when the team has strong shared context, but risks losing the "why" if not disciplined about articulating value. Always pair with clear acceptance criteria.
✓ Good example
"Returning customers can save and reuse delivery addresses at checkout."
Clear, direct, user-focused. The acceptance criteria fill in the detail.
⚠️ Only use plain format when the team has enough shared context to fill in the gaps through conversation. New teams or complex domains benefit from the structure that templates provide.
👤 Personas

Personas & Writing from the User's Perspective

A persona is a research-based representation of a user segment — a fictional character built from real data about the people who use (or will use) your product. Writing stories from a persona's perspective keeps the team anchored in real user behaviour rather than imagined or idealised use cases.

A good persona captures more than demographics. It describes goals, frustrations, behaviours, and context. When the team debates a feature, they should be able to say "would Sarah do this?" — not "would a user do this?"

👩‍💼
Sarah
Busy professional · First-time buyer
Goal
Buy a gift quickly without friction — ideally in under 5 minutes on her phone.
Frustration
Hates being forced to create an account before she can check out.
Context
Usually shops during lunch or on the commute. Always on mobile.
Story angle
"As Sarah, I want to guest checkout so that I don't have to register just to buy one thing."
🧑‍💻
Marcus
Power user · Frequent buyer
Goal
Repeat orders as fast as possible — he buys the same things regularly.
Frustration
Entering the same address and payment details every time is tedious.
Context
Uses desktop, logged in, knows exactly what he wants.
Story angle
"As Marcus, I want one-click reorder so that I can replenish items without re-entering any details."
👴
David
Occasional buyer · Low tech confidence
Goal
Complete a purchase without feeling confused or worried he's done something wrong.
Frustration
Unclear error messages and confusing navigation make him abandon the cart.
Context
Desktop user. Reads every line. Will call support if unsure.
Story angle
"As David, I want clear confirmation at each checkout step so that I know my order is going through correctly."
ℹ️ When the team debates whether a feature belongs in the story, ask "does [persona name] need this to achieve their goal?" It cuts through opinion and returns the conversation to user value. "Would Sarah need this on her five-minute lunch checkout?" is a far more useful question than "should we include this?"

When writing stories from a user's perspective, avoid technical language entirely. The test is: could the persona say this sentence themselves? If the story contains words like "endpoint", "database", or "API", it's been written from a system perspective — not a user one.

✓ User perspective
  • I want to save my delivery address
  • I want to see my order history
  • I want to reset my password via email
  • I want to filter results by price
  • I want to be notified when my order ships
✕ System perspective (avoid)
  • Persist user address to the addresses table
  • Implement GET /api/orders endpoint
  • Send password reset token via SMTP
  • Add price_range filter parameter to search query
  • Trigger Kafka event on shipment status change
✅ Acceptance Criteria

Acceptance Criteria

Acceptance criteria (AC) are the conditions that must be true for a story to be considered complete. They are the "Confirmation" in the 3 C's — the shared definition of done for this specific story. Without AC, neither the team nor the Product Owner has a shared understanding of when the story is finished.

Good AC are precise enough to be testable and concise enough to remain readable. They describe what the system must do — not how it does it. They cover the happy path, key edge cases, and important error states.

✓ Characteristics of good AC
  • Testable — you can write a test or verify it manually
  • Specific — unambiguous, no room for interpretation
  • User-focused — describes observable behaviour, not implementation
  • Complete — covers happy path, error cases, and edge cases
  • Agreed — written with input from PO, dev, and QA
  • Concise — as brief as possible while remaining clear
✕ Signs of weak AC
  • "Works correctly" — not testable
  • "User-friendly" — subjective, not measurable
  • Only the happy path — no edge cases or errors
  • Describes implementation: "validates using regex"
  • Duplicates the story description without adding detail
  • Written by one person without team review
💡 Use the Three Amigos technique: bring the developer, tester, and Product Owner together to write AC collaboratively. Each perspective surfaces different gaps — the developer thinks about implementation constraints, the tester thinks about edge cases, the PO thinks about user intent.
📐 AC Formats

Gherkin vs Listed Acceptance Criteria

There are two dominant formats for writing acceptance criteria: Gherkin (Given/When/Then) and listed criteria (bullet points or numbered conditions). Both are valid — the right choice depends on your team's practices, tooling, and how much precision you need.

DimensionGherkin (Given/When/Then)Listed criteria
StructureScenario-based — one scenario per behaviourCondition-based — one bullet per requirement
ReadabilityVery precise, slightly more verboseFaster to read, less explicit about context
AutomationCan be directly consumed by BDD tools (Cucumber, SpecFlow)Requires manual interpretation for automation
Best forTeams practising BDD, complex multi-step behavioursTeams without BDD tooling, simpler stories
Learning curveModerate — requires discipline to write wellLow — immediately accessible to all stakeholders
RiskCan become overly prescriptive about implementationCan be too vague without enough context

Gherkin format (Given / When / Then)

Gherkin describes behaviour from the user's perspective in a structured, scenario-by-scenario format. It was designed for BDD (Behaviour-Driven Development) and can be consumed directly by automation frameworks.

# Story: Returning customer saves a delivery address
 
Scenario: Customer saves a new address successfully
  Given I am logged in as a registered customer
  And I am on the "My Addresses" page
  When I enter a valid UK address and click "Save"
  Then the address should appear in my saved addresses list
  And I should see a confirmation message "Address saved"
 
Scenario: Customer attempts to save an incomplete address
  Given I am on the "My Addresses" page
  When I submit the form without a postcode
  Then I should see an inline error: "Postcode is required"
  And the address should not be saved

Listed criteria format

Listed criteria are faster to write and easier for non-technical stakeholders to read. They work well for simpler stories or teams without BDD tooling.

Story: Customer saves a delivery address
Acceptance Criteria:
1. A logged-in customer can add a new delivery address from the "My Addresses" page.
2. Required fields: full name, address line 1, city, postcode, country.
3. On successful save, a confirmation message is displayed and the address appears in the list.
4. Incomplete form submissions show inline validation errors for each missing field.
5. A customer can save a maximum of 5 addresses; attempting to add a 6th shows an error.
6. Addresses can be edited and deleted from the list.
7. The address is available for selection at checkout immediately after saving.
ℹ️ You don't have to choose one format for all stories. Use Gherkin for complex, multi-step behaviours where precision matters — and listed criteria for simpler stories. The key is consistency within a story, and agreement across the team on which format to use when.
✓ Best Practices

Best Practices

→ Writing the story
  • Always include the "So that" — value is non-negotiable
  • Name a specific persona, not "a user"
  • Write from the user's perspective, not the system's
  • Keep the title concise — one line, active voice
  • Use the Three Amigos to write AC together
  • Write stories small enough to complete in a sprint
  • Stories should be independently deliverable — no half-features
  • Don't write acceptance criteria for every possible edge case upfront — cover the key ones and let conversation handle the rest
→ Managing stories
  • Keep future stories intentionally vague — detail them only when they're upcoming
  • Stories that can't be completed in a sprint should be split, not stretched
  • A story with no AC is not ready to be pulled into a sprint
  • Review stories with QA before sprint planning — they find the gaps
  • Link stories to a parent epic or goal so context is always visible
  • Close stories that will never be built — a backlog graveyard slows everyone down
💡 The INVEST criteria are the quality check for every story: Independent, Negotiable, Valuable, Estimable, Small, Testable. If a story fails any of these, it needs work before it enters a sprint. See the DoR & DoD Examples for a full INVEST reference.
⚠️ Anti-patterns

Anti-patterns & Pitfalls to Avoid

Most story-writing problems follow predictable patterns. Recognising these anti-patterns early — in refinement, not in production — is one of the highest-value skills a team can develop.

The Missing "So That" No value
As a user, I want to be able to filter results.
Add the reason: "...so that I can quickly find products within my budget." Without the "so that", there's no basis for prioritisation or scope decisions.
The Generic Persona Vague
As a user / As an admin / As the system...
Replace "user" with a named persona. "As Sarah, our first-time mobile buyer..." The specificity forces the team to think about whose behaviour they're actually designing for.
The Technical Story System perspective
As a developer, I want to refactor the address service to use the repository pattern so that the codebase is cleaner.
Technical work should be in an enabler or tech debt story — not a user story. If there's no user value, it shouldn't be in user story format. Use a plain task or spike instead.
The Epic Disguised as a Story Too large
As a customer, I want to be able to manage my account so that I can update all my personal information, preferences, and order history.
Split into independent deliverable stories: "...update my email address", "...set notification preferences", "...view my order history". Each should be completable in a sprint.
The Testless Story No AC
As a user, I want the checkout to be fast. (Acceptance criteria: none)
Every story must have at least one testable acceptance criterion. "Fast" isn't testable — "Checkout completes in under 3 seconds on a standard 4G connection" is.
The Solution Story Prescribes implementation
As a user, I want a dropdown with a checkbox for each category so that I can filter by multiple categories at once.
Separate the need from the solution: "As a user, I want to filter by multiple categories so that I can narrow results to what I'm looking for." Let the team decide how to implement it.
The Conjunction Story Multiple stories
As a user, I want to add, edit, and delete saved addresses so that I can keep my details up to date.
Watch for "and" in the "I want" clause — it's usually two or three stories. Split: "...add a saved address", "...edit a saved address", "...delete a saved address". Each can then be sized, prioritised, and delivered independently.
The Waterfall Story Horizontal slice
Frontend story: Build the address form UI. Backend story: Build the address API. Database story: Create addresses table.
Layer-by-layer stories can't be independently delivered or demoed. Slice vertically: one story that delivers end-to-end "save an address" — even minimally. Technical tasks can exist inside the story, not as separate stories.
⚠️ The most expensive stories are the ones that look finished on paper but were never understood the same way by everyone involved. Invest in the conversation — before, during, and after writing. The card is the beginning of the story, not the end of it.
