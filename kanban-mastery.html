🔄 Overview

What is Kanban?

Kanban is a method for managing knowledge work with an emphasis on just-in-time delivery and continuous flow. Unlike Scrum, it prescribes no fixed roles, no sprints, and no timeboxes. Instead it asks a team to visualise their existing workflow, limit the amount of work in progress, and manage and improve flow over time.

The name comes from the Japanese word for "signboard" or "visual card." It originated in Toyota's manufacturing plants in the 1940s as a scheduling system that pulled work through a production line. David Anderson adapted these principles for knowledge work in the 2000s, formalising what is now known as the Kanban Method.

Stop starting, start finishing. — A core Kanban maxim

Kanban is best understood through its six core practices — not rules imposed from outside, but disciplines that emerge from understanding flow:

01
Visualise the workflow
Make work visible. If you can see it, you can manage it.
02
Limit WIP
Reduce the number of items in progress at any one time to improve flow and reduce cycle time.
03
Manage flow
Monitor, measure, and report on flow to identify improvement opportunities.
04
Make policies explicit
Define and publish the rules of your system so everyone understands how work moves through it.
05
Implement feedback loops
Create regular cadences to inspect and adapt at multiple levels of the system.
06
Improve collaboratively
Use models and the scientific method to drive evolutionary improvement together.
👁️ Visualise

Visualising Flow

A Kanban board makes work visible. Each column represents a state in the workflow — from "To Do" through to "Done." Cards represent individual work items moving through those states. The board surfaces the real state of the system: what's moving, what's blocked, and where work is piling up.

Good boards reflect reality — not a sanitised version of it. Blocked items should be marked. Bottlenecks should be obvious. The goal is not a pretty board but an honest one.

Beyond the basic board, teams often add swim lanes (horizontal rows for different work types or classes of service), explicit queue columns between active columns, and policies written directly on the board — such as the definition of "done" for each column transition.

💡 Start with your actual workflow — not an idealised one. Map what work items actually go through, including rework loops and waiting states. You can always simplify once you understand the real flow.
🚦 WIP Limits

WIP Limits

Work in Progress (WIP) limits are the most powerful — and most resisted — practice in Kanban. They cap the number of items that can be in any given column or stage at one time. When a column is full, the team must finish something before starting something new.

Cycle Time = WIP ÷ Throughput
Little's Law: reducing WIP directly reduces cycle time, even without increasing throughput. The maths is on your side.
✓ Low WIP — healthy flow

Work flows through quickly. Items finish before new ones start. Blockers are visible and resolved fast.

✕ High WIP — congestion

In Progress

Everything is started, nothing is finished. Context switching kills focus. Cycle times balloon. Nobody knows what the priority is.

Setting WIP limits isn't an exact science. A common starting point is 1–1.5 times the number of people working in that stage. Set limits that feel slightly uncomfortable — if nobody ever breaches them, they're too high.

🔄 When a WIP limit is breached, the right response is to swarm on finishing existing work — not to raise the limit. A WIP breach is a signal that something needs attention, not a permission slip to keep starting.
📊 Metrics

Flow Metrics

Kanban is inherently data-driven. Rather than estimating future work in story points, Kanban teams measure historical flow data to make probabilistic forecasts. Four metrics form the core of flow-based measurement:

⏱️
Cycle Time
Start → Done
How long it takes to complete one item once work begins. The primary metric for delivery predictability.
📬
Lead Time
Request → Done
Total elapsed time from request to delivery, including queue time. The metric customers care about.
📦
Throughput
Items / time period
How many items the team completes per week or sprint. Used for capacity forecasting without estimation.
🗂️
WIP
Items in progress now
The number of active items at any point. Reducing it is the fastest lever for improving cycle time.
💡 Use the 85th percentile of cycle time when making delivery commitments — not the average. Averages lie. The 85th percentile means roughly 85% of similar items will complete within that time, giving you a reliable SLA to share with stakeholders.
🌱 Improvement

Continuous Improvement in Kanban

Kanban is an evolutionary method — it doesn't ask you to blow up your current process and start over. Instead it asks you to start with what you have, make it visible, and improve continuously from there. This is sometimes called a Kaizen mindset — small, incremental, sustained improvements over time.

Improvement in Kanban happens at two levels: operational (fixing day-to-day flow problems as they arise) and systemic (regularly stepping back to examine the whole system and make deliberate changes). Both require data, honest conversation, and a culture of experimentation.

Start with what you have
Respect current process
Don't discard existing roles, titles, or processes. Visualise them first, then improve them incrementally.
Make problems visible
Surface blockers immediately
A blocked card on the board is a gift — it tells you where the system is broken before it becomes a crisis.
Use data to decide
Measure before changing
Changes to WIP limits, workflow stages, or policies should be driven by flow data, not gut feel.
Experiment and learn
Run time-boxed experiments
Treat process changes as hypotheses. Run them for 2–4 weeks, measure the impact, and decide whether to keep them.
⚠️ The biggest improvement trap in Kanban is identifying the same bottleneck every week without taking action. If the blockers column is always full, that's systemic — it needs a root cause conversation, not just a daily acknowledgement.
📅 Practices

Common Kanban Practices & Cadences

While Kanban has no mandatory events, it does recommend a set of regular cadences — feedback loops at different time horizons that keep the system healthy and improving. Teams can adopt as many or as few as they need.

☀️
Daily Standup (Flow Focus)
Daily · 15 minutes · Whole team
⌄
In Kanban the daily standup walks the board right-to-left — starting from the items nearest to done. The focus is on flow, not individual status reports. The team asks: what is blocking items from moving forward? What can we finish today?
✓ Tips
  • Walk the board right-to-left — items closest to done get attention first
  • Focus on blocked items and WIP breaches, not individual updates
  • Keep it to 15 minutes — defer problem-solving to after
  • Use the board as the agenda, not a list of questions
✕ Pitfalls
  • Reporting to a manager instead of coordinating as a team
  • Ignoring WIP breaches without discussing them
  • Walking left-to-right and spending all the time on "in progress"
📥
Replenishment Meeting
Weekly or bi-weekly · 30–60 minutes · PO / team lead + team
⌄
The replenishment meeting (also called a queue replenishment) is where the team pulls new work into the system. Items are selected based on priority, capacity, and downstream demand — not a fixed sprint commitment. This is the Kanban equivalent of sprint planning, but it happens continuously rather than at fixed intervals.
✓ Tips
  • Only pull in items that meet the Definition of Ready
  • Let downstream capacity drive pull — don't push work onto the team
  • Prioritise using a clear, visible criteria (value, risk, date, class of service)
  • Keep the meeting short by maintaining a well-refined ready queue
✕ Pitfalls
  • Pulling in more work than capacity supports just to "fill the sprint"
  • Adding unrefined items that aren't truly ready
  • Skipping it and letting work self-assign informally
📊
Operations Review
Monthly · 60–90 minutes · Team + stakeholders
⌄
The Operations Review is a data-driven meeting where the team reviews flow metrics over a longer time horizon — typically the past month. It focuses on throughput trends, cycle time distribution, blocked item patterns, and whether the system is getting faster or slower. It's the Kanban equivalent of a Sprint Review but at a systemic level.
✓ Tips
  • Lead with data — cycle time scatter plots, throughput run charts, cumulative flow diagrams
  • Invite stakeholders who can act on systemic blockers
  • Focus on patterns and trends, not individual items
  • Identify one or two systemic improvements to pursue
✕ Pitfalls
  • Running it as a status update rather than an improvement session
  • No metrics — just anecdotes and feelings about how the month went
  • Identifying problems but not assigning owners or actions
🤝
Service Delivery Review
Bi-weekly or monthly · 30–60 minutes · Team + customers/requesters
⌄
The Service Delivery Review focuses on the relationship between the team and its customers or upstream requesters. It reviews how well the team is meeting its service level expectations — are items completing within their SLAs? Are customers satisfied? Are requests clearly defined when they arrive? This is about the external interface of the system.
✓ Tips
  • Share cycle time data and SLA attainment with customers — transparency builds trust
  • Discuss item quality at intake — are requests arriving ready to work?
  • Use this to align on priorities and Classes of Service
  • Keep it collaborative — not a complaint session
✕ Pitfalls
  • Letting it become a ticket-by-ticket status update
  • Not including actual customers or requesters
  • Focusing only on what went wrong rather than what to improve
🔍
Retrospective (Kaizen Meeting)
Regular cadence · 60 minutes · Team
⌄
Kanban teams still run retrospectives — sometimes called Kaizen meetings — to reflect on how the system is working and identify evolutionary improvements. Unlike Scrum retros which are sprint-bound, Kanban retros are triggered by data (e.g. a spike in cycle time, a class of WIP breaches) or run on a regular cadence. The focus is always on the system, not individuals.
✓ Tips
  • Anchor the retro in flow data — don't let it become purely anecdotal
  • Focus on one or two root-cause experiments rather than a long action list
  • Use PhoenixRetro to facilitate and capture votes on the most important items
  • Track the outcome of past experiments before starting new ones
✕ Pitfalls
  • Running a retro with no data to anchor the conversation
  • Too many actions with no follow-through
  • Blaming individuals rather than examining the system
⚖️ Comparison

Kanban vs Scrum

Kanban and Scrum are both agile frameworks but they differ significantly in structure. Neither is universally better — the right choice depends on the nature of the work and the team. Many teams use elements of both (sometimes called "Scrumban").

AspectScrumKanban
CadenceFixed sprints (1–4 weeks)Continuous flow, no fixed sprints
RolesProduct Owner, Scrum Master, DevelopersNo prescribed roles
PlanningSprint Planning at start of each sprintOngoing replenishment as capacity allows
CommitmentSprint goal and sprint backlogWIP limits only — no sprint commitment
Change mid-cycleProtected during the sprintCan add or reprioritise at any time
EstimationUsually required (story points)Optional — throughput-based forecasting preferred
MetricsVelocity, burndownCycle time, throughput, WIP, CFD
Best forProduct development with regular feature releasesOperations, support, and continuous delivery teams
ℹ️ Many teams successfully combine both — using Scrum's sprint cadence for planning and review while applying Kanban's WIP limits and flow metrics internally. There's no rule against it, and the combination (Scrumban) can be very effective for teams that need structure and flow flexibility.
