⚠️ Common pitfalls

Why Retros Fail

Most teams have sat through a retrospective that felt pointless — the same complaints raised, the same platitudes offered, the same action items written down and forgotten. Understanding why retros fail is the first step to running better ones.

✕ The most common failure modes
  • No follow-up on previous action items — trust erodes fast
  • The same format every sprint — people disengage
  • Management in the room — honest conversation shuts down
  • Venting without root cause analysis or actions
  • Actions assigned to no one — "the team" isn't a person
  • Cancelled when the sprint went well ("nothing to discuss")
  • Running long with no time discipline — energy drops
  • Facilitator also participating as a team member
✓ Signs of a healthy retro
  • People say things they don't normally say in other meetings
  • Actions are specific, named, and followed up next sprint
  • The team leaves energised, not drained
  • Format changes regularly — it doesn't feel rote
  • Issues get resolved over time — not recycled indefinitely
  • Everyone contributes — not just the loudest voices
⚠️ If the team is raising the same issue three sprints in a row, the actions from previous retros aren't working — not because the team isn't trying, but because the root cause hasn't been addressed. Escalate it. Don't let it sit in retro notes indefinitely.
🛡️ Foundation

Psychological Safety First

A retrospective is only as valuable as the honesty it generates. If people don't feel safe raising real issues — because they fear judgment, blame, or political consequences — they'll offer safe, surface-level observations and the retro becomes a formality.

The Safety Check (attributed to Norman Kerth) is a simple way to measure how safe the team feels before the retro starts. Ask each person to anonymously rate their safety on a scale of 1–5:

1Not safeI'll smile and go along with whatever is said but won't contribute.
2GuardedI'll talk about some things but will avoid topics that might get me in trouble.
3MixedI can talk about most things but some issues require a more private setting.
4SafeI can talk about most things with the group — only a few things need to stay private.
5Very safeI feel completely safe — I can say anything that's on my mind.

If the average is below 3, consider addressing the underlying cause before proceeding with a standard retro — a 1-on-1 or smaller group conversation may be more effective.

💡 Prime directive: "Regardless of what we discover, we understand and truly believe that everyone did the best job they could, given what they knew at the time, their skills and abilities, the resources available, and the situation at hand." — Norman Kerth. Read this aloud at the start. It sets the tone.
📐 Preparation

Preparation

A well-facilitated retrospective starts before the meeting begins. Ten minutes of preparation saves thirty minutes of confusion and produces far better outcomes.

→ Before the retro
  • Review action items from the previous retro — know which were completed
  • Choose a format appropriate to the team's current state and energy
  • Set up the board (PhoenixRetro, sticky notes, or whiteboard)
  • Block the time — 60–90 mins for a two-week sprint, 30 for mini-retros
  • Confirm who is attending — no managers unless agreed by the whole team
  • Consider gathering data: sprint metrics, blockers log, incident count
✓ Facilitator mindset
  • Your job is to guide the process, not drive the content
  • Stay neutral — don't advocate for your own views
  • Watch for quiet people — draw them in gently
  • Watch the energy — know when to speed up or slow down
  • If you're a team member too, consider having someone else facilitate
📅 Structure

The Five Phases

Esther Derby and Diana Larsen's framework from Agile Retrospectives (2006) breaks every retrospective into five phases. Not every retro needs all five in depth, but skipping phases — especially Set the Stage and Close — is a common cause of poor outcomes.

1
Set the Stage
5–10 minutes · Creates focus and safety
⌄
The opening phase gets everyone present, sets expectations, and establishes psychological safety. It reminds the team of the purpose of the retro and the working agreement for the session. Never skip this — even a two-minute check-in changes the energy of the room.
✓ Activities
  • Read the Prime Directive aloud
  • Run a safety check (1–5 anonymous vote)
  • Quick check-in: one word describing how you feel right now
  • State the goal of this specific retro ("today we're focusing on…")
→ Watch for
  • People already on their laptops — bring them back
  • Low safety scores — address before proceeding
  • Jumping straight to problems — enforce the stage
2
Gather Data
10–15 minutes · Facts and feelings about the sprint
⌄
Before the team can improve, they need a shared picture of what actually happened. This phase gathers both objective data (what events occurred, metrics, blockers) and subjective data (how people felt). Different perspectives are surfaced — including things the facilitator may not know about.
✓ Activities
  • Timeline: plot key events from the sprint on a shared timeline
  • Team Radar: rate dimensions like collaboration, quality, and process
  • Mad / Sad / Glad: emotional data alongside factual data
  • Metrics review: velocity, escaped defects, blockers count
→ Watch for
  • Only surfacing negatives — positive data matters too
  • People filtering themselves — encourage all observations
  • Jumping to solutions before understanding what happened
3
Generate Insights
15–20 minutes · Understanding why, not just what
⌄
This is the most intellectually demanding phase — moving from "what happened" to "why it happened." The team analyses patterns in the data, identifies root causes, and surfaces the systemic issues behind surface-level symptoms. Without this phase, actions treat symptoms rather than causes.
✓ Activities
  • 5 Whys: ask "why?" five times to find the root cause
  • Fishbone diagram: map causes across people, process, tools, environment
  • Dot voting: identify the highest-priority issues to focus on
  • Affinity grouping: cluster related observations into themes
→ Watch for
  • Staying at symptom level — push for root causes
  • Blaming individuals — redirect to systems and processes
  • One voice dominating the analysis — facilitate equal contribution
4
Decide What to Do
10–15 minutes · Concrete, owned actions
⌄
The insight phase generates understanding — this phase converts it into commitments. The team selects the most impactful improvements and defines specific, owned actions. Quality over quantity: one well-defined action that actually gets done is worth more than five that don't.
✓ Activities
  • SMART actions: Specific, Measurable, Achievable, Relevant, Time-bound
  • Prioritise to 1–3 actions maximum — more than that rarely get done
  • Assign a named owner to each action — not "the team"
  • Add actions to the sprint backlog or team board — make them visible
→ Watch for
  • Agreeing to actions that require someone outside the team to change
  • Vague actions: "improve communication" — what does that mean?
  • No owner assigned — it won't happen
  • Too many actions — narrow focus produces better results
5
Close the Retrospective
5–10 minutes · Appreciation and feedback
⌄
The closing phase grounds the team, celebrates what was surfaced, and gives the facilitator feedback on the retro itself. It's often skipped — which means the team leaves on whatever emotional note the actions discussion ended on, rather than on a constructive, energised note.
✓ Activities
  • Appreciation round: each person thanks one teammate for something specific
  • Retro on the retro: one word or emoji to describe the session
  • ROTI (Return on Time Invested): 1–5 vote on whether it was worth the time
  • Recap the actions and owners — confirm shared understanding
→ Watch for
  • Rushing through closing when time runs short — protect it
  • Skipping appreciation — it matters for team cohesion
  • Not asking for feedback on the facilitation — how else will you improve?
🎨 Formats

Formats & Techniques

Rotating formats keeps retrospectives fresh and draws out different types of insight. Choose based on the team's current energy, the sprint's character, and what hasn't been done recently. All of these are available as templates in PhoenixRetro.

😊😡😢
Happy / Mad / Sad
Emotional check
Captures the emotional temperature of the sprint. Good for teams who need to surface feelings alongside facts. Simple and inclusive — anyone can contribute regardless of role.
⭐
Starfish
Behaviour change
Five columns: Start, Stop, Continue, More of, Less of. Produces nuanced, action-oriented output. Better than simple "good/bad" formats because it captures gradations of behaviour.
💡📚❌🌟
4Ls
Learning focus
Liked, Learned, Lacked, Longed for. Particularly effective when the team has been through something new — a new process, technology, or team member joining.
▶️ ⏹️ ⏩
Start / Stop / Continue
Action-focused
The simplest action-oriented format. Three clear categories that generate immediately actionable output. Good for time-constrained retros or teams new to retrospectives.
🧲
KALM
Nuanced change
Keep, Add, Less, More. More nuanced than Start/Stop/Continue because "Less" and "More" acknowledge that things can be good but need calibrating, not eliminating.
🧞
Genie in a Bottle
Creative energy
What went well, what held us back, and what you'd wish for. The "wish" framing unlocks more ambitious thinking — teams often surface systemic issues that other formats miss.
✅ Follow-through

Turning Talk into Action

The retrospective is worthless without follow-through. The most common reason teams stop trusting retros is that action items from previous sessions are never completed. Here's how to break that cycle:

✓ Making actions stick
  • Add retro actions to the sprint backlog — they get the same visibility as stories
  • Assign a single named owner — not "the team" or "everyone"
  • Make them SMART: "by end of sprint 14, Alex will set up automated linting in the pipeline"
  • Open the next retro by reviewing last sprint's actions — before anything else
  • Limit to 1–3 actions — more than that dilutes focus
  • Celebrate when actions are completed — it reinforces the loop
✕ Action anti-patterns
  • "Talk to X about Y" — a conversation isn't an outcome
  • "Improve our testing" — not specific enough to be actionable
  • Actions that require someone outside the team to change without their agreement
  • Long lists of actions that overwhelm the team each sprint
  • No follow-up mechanism — actions disappear into notes
ℹ️ If the same issue keeps appearing in retrospectives without resolution, it's likely a systemic problem outside the team's direct control. Escalate it to leadership, your Scrum Master, or the Product Owner rather than continuing to generate actions that the team can't actually deliver on.
💻 Remote

Remote Retrospectives

Remote retrospectives require more deliberate facilitation than in-person ones. The informal social cues that help a facilitator gauge the room — body language, side conversations, energy levels — are mostly invisible online. You have to compensate with better structure and more explicit check-ins.

→ Remote best practices
  • Use a digital board — PhoenixRetro, Miro, or FunRetro — so everyone can contribute simultaneously
  • Cameras on where possible — reading the room still matters
  • Use anonymous digital voting to ensure honest input
  • Build in more silence than you're comfortable with — give people time to write
  • Use breakout rooms for small-group discussions before bringing the full team together
  • Keep it shorter — remote meetings are more draining
→ Watch for
  • One person dominating because others are reluctant to interrupt
  • Participants multitasking — a tight agenda and direct questions help
  • Technical issues eating into retro time — test tools beforehand
  • Silence being misread as agreement — explicitly check in
💡 For distributed teams across time zones, consider asynchronous contribution before the live session — team members add notes to the board at their own time, then the live session focuses on discussion and prioritisation rather than note-writing.
